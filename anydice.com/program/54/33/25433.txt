\In this system, adding a dice represents an improved ability to affect ones environment in their desired manner, a failure represents some complication with that\
\And a new mechanic represents pushing one's own change in the environment over other actors: the unlocking mechanism
With an unlock, as long as you gain the 1 success you need to unlock it, you can count more numbers for success against an opponent's successes, or against a target number of successes
Your odds of affecting your environment in your desired manner remain unchanged, but your ability to override increases drastically\
\Proficiency in a skill grants natural success on menial tasks related to that skill, particularly tasks conducted during calm or casual moments\

\With my current idea for how bonuses in contest rolls, someone having a greater aptitude for using a particular trait to affect their environment has a drastically better chance at winning the contest than someone with a lower aptitude; and it gets exponentially worse as the gap widens\
\It also means that a single bonus drastically improves your chances to beat someone with even one less bonus; the odds are incredibly stacked in your favor; for players vs NPCs this actually works out great, players gain character motivation to impose their will over someone else, and they get a big reward for it; for player vs player, this also works out well, because the player with the most investment will have a much greater chance of victory\
\The question to ask is, do I want higher rates of difficulty for realizing one's desired change in the environment than what the d4 provides. But I posit this, the difficulty in realizing the change you want isn't completely up to the dice. In fact, a lot of it isn't up to the dice. The goal of the game is for most of that to be up to the in game story. So, I'm fine with the d4s rates\
